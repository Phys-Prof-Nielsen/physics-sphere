D4.1 Work-Energy Theorem#

D4.1.1 Motivation#

We have previously defined motion through momentum

\[\vec{p} = m\vec{v} \]

and then determined momentum transfer or impulse as a given force acting on the particle through time

\[ \vec{i} = \int_{t_1}^{t_{2}}\vec{f}dt \]

The net impulse was the change in momentum of the particle, which defined the impulse-momentum theorem

\[ \int_{t_1}^{t_{2}}\vec{f}_{net}dt = \Delta\vec{p} \]

The impulse-momentum theorem reduces to the more commonly known Newton’s 2nd law, where the interactions between two objects are in focus.

We notice that in the above, we must have knowledge of the force as a function of time (unless it is a constant force). At times, we do not have such information. Instead, we have knowledge of the force as a function of postion. In these cases, the work-energy concept is more suitable.

D4.1.2 Kinetic Energy#

Just as we defined momentum as a measure of motion, kinetic energy is also a measure of motion. We define the kinetic energy of a particle as

\[ k = \frac{1}{2}m\vec{v}\cdot\vec{v} \]

For a system of particles, we can define the center of mass kinetic energy as

\[ K = \frac{1}{2}M\vec{V}\cdot\vec{V} \]

The kinetic energy of the center of mass is not necessarily the same as the kinetic energy of the system!

D4.1.3 Work#

Work is a measure of energy transfer to/from a particle by a given force and is defined as the path integral

\[ w = \int_L\vec{f}\cdot d\vec{l} \]

D4.1.4 Work-Energy Theorem#

The net work is the net kinetic energy transfer and is establisged through the work-energy theorem

\[ \int_L\vec{f}_{net}\cdot d\vec{l} = \Delta k\]

It should be pointed that the impulse-momentum and work-energy theorems are consistent in the sense that one can be derived from the other. We will do that later in the course. It also leads to a somewhat philosophical question: what concept is more fundamental? Force or Energy?. In Newtonian mechanics force is certainly the more fundamental and based on observations. We will later see how a more rigorous energy approach leads to modern field theories and are based on a fundamental principle of nature.

Box 1

Consider a block of mass \(m\) attached to a spring with a spring constant \(k\) on a frictionless horizontal surface. Derive an expression for the position of the block using the work-energy theorem.

NOTE: This is a standard problem demonstrating simple harmonic motion. It is usually solved through Newton’s 2nd law leading to the 2nd order simple harmonic differential equation and found in almost every introductory textbook. This is also in Kleppner and Kolenkow (Example 5.2). Pay attention to the quote: The integral is listed in standard tables. It can also be generated by symbolic mathematical routines, or by the time-honored methods of “guesswork” and “playing around.” Using these methods is as respectable for a physicist as con- sulting a dictionary is for a writer. Of course, in both cases one hopes that experience gradually reduces dependence. That book is a detailed must-read for any physicists :)

Box 2

A particle is moved around counterclockwise a full rectangular path with vertices at \((x,y) = (0,0), (3,0), (3,2), (0,2)\) m. The force is given by

\[ \vec{f} = 3.5xy^2\hat{i} - 5.0y\hat{j} \]

What is the work done by this force in moving the particle around the given path.

%reset -f

import sympy as sym

x0 = 0.0
y0 = 0.0
x1 = 3.0
y1 = 0.0
x2 = 3.0
y2 = 2.0
x3 = 0.0
y3 = 2.0

x = sym.Symbol('x')
y = sym.Symbol('y')

fx = 3.5*x*y**2
fy = -5.0*y

# Path 1
w1 = sym.integrate(fx,(x,x0,x1)) + sym.integrate(fy,(y,y0,y1))
w1 = w1.evalf(subs={y:y0}) 

# Path 2
w2 = sym.integrate(fx,(x,x1,x2)) + sym.integrate(fy,(y,y1,y2))
w2 = w2.evalf(subs={x:x1}) 

# Path 3
w3 = sym.integrate(fx,(x,x2,x3)) + sym.integrate(fy,(y,y2,y3))
w3 = w3.evalf(subs={y:y2})

# Path 4
w4 = sym.integrate(fx,(x,x3,x0)) + sym.integrate(fy,(y,y3,y0))
w4 = w4.evalf(subs={x:x3})

w = w1 + w2 + w3 + w4

print('Total work done is '+str(w)+' J.')

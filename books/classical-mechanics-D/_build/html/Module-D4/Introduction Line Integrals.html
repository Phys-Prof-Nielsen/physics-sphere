Introduction to Line Integrals#

Sometimes we need new tools to expand our capabilities in physics. In this module, we will introduce the line integrals and partial derivatives. Both are items covered in a third semester of calculus. Our main objectives in this course can be addressed without these new concepts, but it is like eating cake without ice cream. My objective for introducing them to you is: it is essential that you have a complete understanding of line integrals and partial derivatives when you enter the next level of physics. However, being introduced to it in Calculus III often leaves students with just basic application skills but very little conceptual understanding and extensive practice. Hence, the comfort level when entering Modern Physics and Classical Mechanics and/or advanced engineering courses is low and reduces the learning outcome in those classes as focus shift from the physics to the mathematical approach. By introducing the concepts and basic application level now, it is my hope that you will gain more from your Calculus III exposure and be fluent in the skills as you enter your next level of physics or engineering.

So just to be clear, Calculus III is not required for this class, and I will not expect mastering of these concepts from this class, but simply expose you to them. As with the rest of the material, we will continue using it and as we progress in the semester, we will gain more confidence and ability to use it.

Line integrals is used to define and calculate mechanical work, but also used to evaulate electric and magnetic fields through Faraday’s and Ampere’s laws and are part of Maxwell’s equations.

Line Integral of Vector Fields#

From Calculus I, we have learned how to calculate the integral of a function as it varies with a single variable (in physics, often position \(x\) or time \(t\)). For the position variable, that implies we are integrating over a straight line segment. For example, the work done by a force \(\vec{F}\) acting on an object that is moving over a displacement \(\Delta \vec{x} = \vec{x}_2 - \vec{x}_1\) is:

\[ W = \int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{x} \]

or with the dot product written in geometric form using magnitudes of the vectors and relativive orientation:

\[ W = \int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} F\cos\theta dx \]

where \(F\) and \(dx\) are the respective magnitudes and \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors. What this integral represent is actually a line integral over a vector field for a 1D line segment.

In general, the line integral is defined as a scaled vector projection of a vector \(\vec{F}\) along some arbitrary path \(L\). If an infinitesimal directional path length of \(L\) is \(d\vec{l}\), then the line integral (or work) is:

\[ W = \int_L \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{l} \]

where \(L\) simply implies that we are integrating along a path described by \(L\).

Since we are not familiar with integrating vectors, we can write the dot product out in two ways. First, using the components of the two vectors (assuming we are in cartesian coordinates, which just happens to be our choice of place in mpost of Physics I and II. The next level up, Classical Mechanics, we will go outside the cartesian room and see how big the world really is):

\[ W = \int_L (F_{x}dx + F_{y}dy + F_{z}dz)\]

We can consider this a sum of integrals along each cartesian direction and specify the limits of integration:

\[ W = \int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} F_{x}dx + \int_{y_{1}}^{y_{2}} F_{y}dy + \int_{z_{1}}^{z_{2}} F_{z}dz\]

We note the following:

  • We converted our integrals involving vectors into three 1D scalar integrals, which are familiar with from Calculus I. Yah for us!

  • The scalar quantities are the vector components of \(\vec{F}\) and \(d\vec{l}\), respectively, and not the magnitudes.

Example 1#

Consider your massive physics textbook with a mass of \(2.18\)~kg. Its original position is \(\vec{r}_0 = (0,0)\). By applying a push force with a constant magnitude of \(5.0\) N, we first slide it along \(+x\) followed by motion along \(+y\) to a final destination of \(\vec{r} = (0.3~\textrm{m},0.2~\textrm{m})\). Our path \(L\) then takes the shape of two sides of a rectangle. What is the work done by our applied force?

Solution#

According to our definition of work

\[ W =\int_L \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{l} \]

we can split this into vector component form:

\[ W =\int_L (F_{x}dx + F_{y}dy) \]

and re-write it as two integrals with the explicit limits:

\[ W =\int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} F_{x}dx + \int_{y_{1}}^{y_{2}} F_{y}dy \]

Inserting the numbers provided, we get

\[ W = \int_{0}^{0.3} (5.0)dx + \int_{0}^{0.2} (5.0)dy \]
\[ = [(5.0)x]_{0}^{0.3} + [(5.0)y]_{0}^{0.2}\]

and our final result is

\[ W = +2.5~\textrm{J} \]

This example was not too bad, was it? Of course, sometimes breaking it into component form is not pretty and leaves some nasty integrals. Let us try a more complicated example, where we can see that we may have been a little sloppy in our work but getting away with the crime.

Example 2#

Consider a force given by \(\vec{F} = 5.0(xy\hat{i} + y^2\hat{j})\) acting on an object. Over two experiments, the object moves from \(\vec{r}_i = (0,0)\) to \(\vec{r}_f = (1,1)\) along two paths: Path 1: along \(+x\) followed by \(+y\) and Path 2: along \(+y\) followed by \(+x\).

Solution#

Path 1 Writing out the line integral from definition and into component to gain experience. This step can be skipped as we gain experience:

\[ W_1 = \int_L \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{l} \]
\[ W = \int_L (F_{x}dx + F_{y}dy) \]
\[ W = \int_{x_{1}}^{x_{2}} F_{x}dx ~+~ \int_{y_{1}}^{y_{2}} F_{y}dy \]

It is no evident that we have broken our path L into two segments (integrals): one segment along the x-axis and a second segment along the y-axis.

Now, we have to be careful as the \(x\)-componet of the force also depends on \(y\). Hence, in the integral we must be aware of the \(y\)-value as we integrate along the \(x\)-direction. We notice, that the \(y\)-value will be constant. With that in mind, we can write it as

\[ W_1 =\int_{x_{1}=0, y = 0}^{x_{2} = 1, y =0} 5.0xydx ~+~ \int_{y_{1} = 0, x =1}^{y_{2} = 1, x = 1} 5.0y^2dy \]

Since \(y\) is constant and equal to zero in the first term, we get

\[ W_1 = 0 ~+~ \frac{5}{3} \]

and the final answer is

\[ W_1 = + \frac{5}{3}~\textrm{J} \]

Path 2 Similarly for Path 2: $\( W_2 =\int_{x_{1}=0, y = 1}^{x_{2} = 1, y =1} 5.0xydx ~+~ \int_{y_{1} = 0, x = 0}^{y_{2} = 1, x = 0} 5.0y^2dy \)$

\[ = \frac{5}{2} ~+~ \frac{5}{3} \]

and the final answer is

\[ W_2 = \frac{25}{6}~\textrm{J} \]

We notice that the amount of work required for each path is different, which is a characteristic of a non-conservative force.

IMPORTANT Review from Physics I: We notice in the above example, that the work done by the force in moving the object from point $A$ to point $B$ depended on the chosen path. Such forces are referred to as non-conservative forces. Forces where the work in going from point $A$ to point $B$ does not depend on the chosen path are referred to as conservative forces. Conservative forces are responsible for transferring energy from one form to another within a system and associated with potential energy functions (examples: gravity and spring forces), whereas non-conservative forces are responsible for transferring energy into or out of a system (examples: pushing/pull forces and friction).
Box 1
Consider a force given by $\vec{F} = 5.0[x^2y^2\hat{i} + (x^2 + y^2)\hat{j}]$ acting on an object. Over two experiments, the object moves from $\vec{r}_i = (0,0)$ to $\vec{r}_f = (1~\textrm{m},1~\textrm{m})$ along two paths: Path 1: along $+x$ followed by $+y$ and Path 2: along $+y$ followed by $+x$. What is the work done by this force along the two different paths? Comment on your result. Answers: $\frac{20}{3}$ J and $\frac{10}{3}$ J
Box 2
Consider a force given by $\vec{F} = 5.0(xy\hat{i} + y^2\hat{j})$ acting on an object. The object moves from $\vec{r}_i = (0,0)$ to $\vec{r}_f = (1~\textrm{m},1~\textrm{m})$ along $+x$ followed by $+y$. Then we bring it back to its original position along $-x$ and $-y$. What is the work done? Is the applied force conservative or non-conservative? Explain. Numerical Answer: $-\frac{5}{2}$ J

In this last example, we integrated along a path where the start and end points were the same location. This belongs to a special group of line integrals and we write them as:

\[ W = \oint_L \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{l} \]

where the circle indicates a closed path \(L\).

The message from our previous info Box was really important, so let us repeat it here and add to it:

Review from Physics I: We notice in the above example, that the work done by the force in moving the object from point $A$ to point $B$ depended on the chosen path. Such forces are referred to as non-conservative forces. Forces where the work in going from point $A$ to point $B$ does not depend on the chosen path are referred to as conservative forces. Conservative forces are responsible for transferring energy from one form to another within a system and associated with potential energy functions (examples: gravity and spring forces), whereas non-conservative forces are responsible for transferring energy into or out of a system (examples: pushing/pull forces and friction).

Another way of defining a conservative force: a conservative force is characterized by doing zero work along a closed path.

This result has a very important consequence as it allows us to define a scalar function known as the potential energy. I hope you remember this from Physics I! If not, you may have realized that we are in the process of doing a mixture of introducing new calculus and refreshing concepts from Physics I.

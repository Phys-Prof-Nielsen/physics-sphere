C2.7 Concept of Displacement Vector#

C2.7.1 1D Displacement Vector#

We will introduce the displacement vector in 1D first and use it to illustrate geometrical addition of vectors in the next section. After that, we will consider the displacement in 2D and 3D.

Consider an object located at x\(_A\) = 3.0 m with position vector \(\vec{x}_A\). Now imagine that the object moves to a new position x\(_B\) = 4.5 m with a new position vector \(\vec{x}_B\) by moving a distance \(\Delta\)x = 1.5 m in the positive direction (NOTE: distance changed + direction = vector : \(\Delta\vec{x}\)). This can be illustrated as

Note, the final position vector can be visualized by adding the two arrows representing \(\vec{x}_A\) and \(\Delta\vec{x}\) together. In Laymanâ€™s terms, we will say that a starting position plus a change results in a new position. In the mathematical language, we would say:

\[ \vec{x}_B = \vec{x}_A + \Delta\vec{x}\]

The change in position vector, \(\Delta\vec{x}\), is known as the displacement vector:

\[ \Delta\vec{x} = \vec{x}_B - \vec{x}_A\]

which in words is stated as: the displacement vector is the final position vector subtracted by the initial position vector. We can state that the displacement vector is describing a change (magnitude and/or direction) in the position of an object.

In this example, we may conclude the following: if we follow the initial position vector \(\vec{x}_A\) from tail to head, then continue along the displacement \(\Delta\vec{x}\), we end up at the tip of the final position vector \(\vec{x}_B\). This is a general rule for geometric addition of vectors, which we will cover in the next section.

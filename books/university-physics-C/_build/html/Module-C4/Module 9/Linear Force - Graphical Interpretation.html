Linear Force: Graphical Interpretation#

1. Area#

We have seen that we can define the net interaction on a point particle through

\[ \Delta\vec{p} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\vec{F}_{net}dt \tag{1}\]

and if the applied net force is constant (or considering the average force over the time interval):

\[ \Delta\vec{p} = \vec{F}_{net}\Delta t \tag{2}\]

and for a system of particles, where we consider the Center of Mass of the system:

\[ \Delta\vec{P} = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\vec{F}_{net}^{ext}dt \tag{3}\]

and if the applied net force is constant (or considering the average force over the time interval):

\[ \Delta\vec{P} = \vec{F}_{net}^{ext}\Delta t \tag{4}\]

where ext refers to external forces acting on the system.

In any of these cases, we can interpretate it as the following:

The impulse (or change in momentum) of a point particle or a center of mass system is the area bounded by the graph and the time-axis in a net force vs. time graph.

Example 1#

A 2.0 kg point particle is subject to a net force vs. time graph as the one shown by executing the code below. Assume all interactions takes place along the x-direction.

  1. What is the linear impulse of the particle over the time interval $t = [0,10]$?
  2. If the particle is at rest at t = 0 s, what is its velocity at t = 10 s? 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

%matplotlib inline

t1 = np.arange(0,5.1,0.1)
F1 = 2.0*np.ones(len(t1))
t2 = np.arange(0,5.1,0.1) + 5.0
F2 = -0.4*t2 + 4.0

plt.figure()
plt.ylabel('Net Force (N)')
plt.xlabel('Time (s)')
ax = plt.gca()
ax.plot(t1,F1,'b')
ax.plot(t2,F2,'b')
ax.set_xlim([0, 10.5])
ax.set_ylim([0, 2.5])
ax.grid()
plt.show()

Solution#

  1. The impulse is the area bounded by the graph and the time axis. We can divide it into two segments: a rectangle and a triangle. The ara of the rectangle is $A_1 = (2.0)(5) = 10$ Ns and the area of the triangle is $A_2 = (0.5)(5)(2.0) = 5.0$ Ns. The total area, and the impulse, is then
    \[\vec{J} = (15~\textrm{Ns})~\hat{i}\]

    NOTE: the unit Ns is the same as kgm/s.

  2. The impulse is the change in momentum of the particle: $$ m\vec{v}_2 - m\vec{v}_1 = \vec{J} $$

    Solving for the final velocity: $\(\vec{v}_2 = \vec{v}_1 + \frac{\vec{J}}{m} \)\( and inserting our numbers: \)\(\vec{v}_2 = (0)~\hat{i} + \frac{(15)~\hat{i}}{(2.0)} = (7.5~\textrm{m/s})~\hat{i} \)$

%matplotlib inline

t = np.arange(0,10.1,0.1)
F = -500.0 + 200*t

plt.ylabel('Net Force (N)')
plt.xlabel('Time (s)')
ax = plt.gca()

ax.set_xlim([0, 10])
ax.set_ylim([-500, np.max(F)])
ax.grid()
plt.show()

Solution:#

IMPORTANT: the graph only specify the change in momentum and we can therefore only say something about the change in velocity provided we know the mass of the object. To say soemthing about final velocity, we must know the initial velocity and mass and vice versa. This is similar to our discussion on velocity and position or acceleration and velocity.

2. Slope#

Through the relationships

\[ \vec{F}_{next} = \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} \]

and

\[ \vec{F}_{next}^{ext} = \frac{d\vec{P}}{dt} \]

we observe that

The net force is represented by the slope of a momentum vs. time graph.

This relationship is rarely practical, and is just something we should keep in the back of our mind in case we stumble upon it.

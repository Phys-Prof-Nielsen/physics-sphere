Problem D4.1

Before a crash-test collision, a car with a crash test dummy of mass 75-kg is traveling at 25.0 m/s towards a wall. The impact time is 10.00 ms. Assume the impact force is much greater than any other forces involved in the collision.

  1. If the crast test dummy is fasten to the car, what impact force is experienced by the dummy?

  2. Instead of being fasten to the car, the dummy is wearing seat belts and the impact time is increased to 20.0 ms. What is the impact force then?

  3. In addition to seat belt, the car is equipped with airbags increasing the impact time to a whooping 0.100 s. What is the impact force in this scenario?

  4. The acceleration of gravity is 9.81 m/s\(^2\). How many g’s did the dummy experience in each crash scenario? HINT: take the acceleration and divide by 9.81 to obtain it in units of g.

%reset -f

import sympy as sym

m = 63.0
ax = 4.200

#declare unknown variable
fx = sym.Symbol('fx')

#equation to solve: 0 = f - ma (comes from f = ma)
eq = fx - m*ax

sol = sym.solve(eq,fx)
fx = sol[0]

Detailed Solutions#

Problem D4.1

Problem D4.1

Problem

Before a crash-test collision, a car with a crash test dummy of mass 75-kg is traveling at 25.0 m/s towards a wall. The impact time is 10.00 ms. Assume the impact force is much greater than any other forces involved in the collision.

  1. If the crast test dummy is fasten to the car, what impact force is experienced by the dummy?

  2. Instead of being fasten to the car, the dummy is wearing seat belts and the impact time is increased to 20.0 ms. What is the impact force then?

  3. In addition to seat belt, the car is equipped with airbags increasing the impact time to a whooping 0.100 s. What is the impact force in this scenario?

  4. The acceleration of gravity is 9.81 m/s\(^2\). How many g’s did the dummy experience in each crash scenario?

Methodology
We assume that the dummy comes to a rest without bouncing off the wall. Then we can combine the defition of momentum

\( p_x = mv_x \)

and the impulse-momentum theorem

\( \Delta p_x = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

to obtain the impact force. Combining the two equations, we get for the net force (the same as the impact force, since any other forces are negligible):

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{mv_{fx} - mv_{ix}}{\Delta t} \)

To obtain the impact forces in g’s, we use \( f_{net,x} = ma_x \) to find the acceleration and scale it by 9.8 to obtain the acceleration in terms of g’s.

Solution
In the following, we will make the initial direction of travel positive.
Part 1:

Pluggin in values:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(75)(0) - (75)(+25.0)}{10.0\times 10^{-3}} = -1.9\times 10^5~\textrm{N}\)

Part 2:

Pluggin in values:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(75)(0) - (75)(+25.0)}{20.0\times 10^{-3}} = -9.4\times 10^4~\textrm{N}\)

Part 3:

Pluggin in values:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(75)(0) - (75)(+25.0)}{0.100} = -1.9\times 10^4~\textrm{N}\)

Part 4:

The acceleration in the first collision is (re-calculate the forces above and carry enough digits through)

\( a_x = \frac{f_{net,x}}{m} = \frac{-1.87500\times 10^5}{75} = -2,500.00~\textrm{m/s}^2 \)

Since \(\frac{1~\textrm{g}}{9.8~\textrm{m/s}^2}\), we have

\( a_x = -260~\textrm{g} \)

Similarly, the other accelerations are:

\( a_x = -130~\textrm{g} \)

and

\( a_x = -26~\textrm{g}\)

Reflection
Any collision resulting in 50 g’s or more are considered to yield serious injuries and potentially fatal. Keep in mind that 25 m/s is equivalent to 90 km/hr or 56 mph, so we are talking about relatively high-speed head-on collision.

Kenny Brack experienced a record-breaking 214 g (peak, not average) during a race car accident in Texas.

Dale Earnhardt experienced a fatal g-force of ~58 g during the 2001 Daytona 500.

Problem D4.2

Problem D4.2

Problem

A 5.0 kg particle is traveling in 1D at -35 m/s when it interacts with another particle. After the interaction the velocity is -5.0 m/s.

  1. Make a statement about the direction of the interaction in terms (positive or negative direction) and explain your answer.

  2. If the magnitude of the net average force during the collision was 3.0E3 N, what was the impact time?

  3. What was the acceleration of the of the particle during the interaction?

Methodology

As in previous problems, we can combine the defition of momentum

\( p_x = mv_x \)

and the impulse-momentum theorem

\( \Delta p_x = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

to obtain the impact time. Combining the two equations, we get for the impact time:

\( \Delta t = \frac{mv_{fx} - mv_{ix}}{f_{net,x}} \)

In the last part, we can use \(F_{net,x} = ma\) to find the acceleration.

Solution
Part 1:

The particle is moving in the negative direction but slowing down, which can only be true if a net interaction (force) is acting in opposite direction: the interaction must be in the positive direction.

Part 2:

Pluggin in values to solve for the impact time:

\( \Delta t = \frac{(5.0)(-5.0) - (5.0)(-35)}{+3.0\times 10^{3}} = +5.0\times 10^{-2}~\textrm{s}\)

Part 3:

Using Newton’s 2nd law and solve for the acceleration:

\( a_x = \frac{f_{net,x}}{m} = \frac{3.0\times 10^3}{5.0} = 6.0\times 10^2~\textrm{m/s}^2 \)

Reflection
Our prediction was correct, the force is in the positive direction since that gave us a positive interaction time.

Problem D4.3

Problem D4.3

Problem

A net force of \(f_x = +5.0\) N is in contact with a 0.50 kg particle over the duration of 8.0 s.

  1. Is momentum transferred to or from the particle? Explain your answer.

  2. What is the momentum transfer experienced by the particle?

  3. If the initial velocity of the particle was +25 m/s, what is the velocity of the particle after the interaction?

  4. What acceleration did the particle experience during the interaction?

Methodology

Since momentum transfer is the same as impulse or the change in momentum, we can apply the impulse-momentum theorem directly:

\( \Delta p_x = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

Using the definition of momentum , we can write the impulse as:

\( \Delta p_x = mv_{fx} - mv_{ix} \)

Combining the two equations allows us to solve for the final velocity:

\( v_{fx} = v_{ix} + \frac{f_{net,x}\Delta t}{m} \)

Since we know the initial velocity, the elapsed time, and the newly gained final velocity, we can calculate the acceleration from definition of acceleration, or we can use Newton’s 2nd law.

Solution
Part 1:

Since the force is positive, the momentum will increase and the momentum transfer must be positive.

Part 2:

Plug in values to solve for the momentum transfer:

\( \Delta p_x = (+5.0)(8.0) = +4.0\times 10^{1}~\textrm{kgm/s}\)

Part 3:

Solving for the final velocity:

\( v_{fx} = (+25) + \frac{+40}{0.50} = +105~\textrm{m/s} \)

Part 4:

Using the definition of acceleration (use non-rounded answer from Part 3):

\( a_{x} = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{(+105)-(+25)}{8.0} = +1.0E1~\textrm{m/s}^2 \)

Reflection
The result in Part 2 validates our statement that momentum is transferred to the particle.

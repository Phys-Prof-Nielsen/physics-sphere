Problem D3.1

Before a crash-test collision, a dummy of mass 75-kg is traveling at +25.0 m/s towards a wall. What is the impulse during the collision?

Show code cell source Hide code cell source 
%reset -f
# declare given parameters
m = 75.0   #mass
v1x = 25.0  # initial x-velocity

# indirect information
# in a crash test, the final velocity is zero
v2x = 0.0
# impulse
ix = m*(v2x - v1x)

# print the answer
print('The impulse experienced by the dummy during the collision is: '+str(ix)+' kgm/s.')

  1. What is the initial velocity of the particle?

  2. What is the initial and final momentums of the particle?

  3. Before calculating the impulse, make a prediction about the direction of impulse. Explain your reasoning.

  4. Calculate the momentum transfer for the particle. (Remember that impulse is momentum transfer.)

%reset -f

v1z = -335.0
v2z = -875.0

# Part 1
# ------
# velocity is speed + direction

Detailed Solutions#

Problem D3.1

Problem D3.1

Problem
Before a crash-test collision, a dummy of mass 75-kg is traveling at +25.0 m/s towards a wall. What is the impulse during the collision?

Methodology
We realized that the final velocity of the dummy is zero, which allows us to plug in values for the impulse. The impulse is the change in momentum. If we refer to the two states as final (f) and initial (i), we have

\( \Delta p_x = p_{fx} - p_{ix} \)

and then we use the definition of momentum

\( p_x = mv_x \)

to find the initial and final momentums.

Solution
Plugging in our numbers, we get

\( \Delta p_x = (75)(0) - (75)(+25.0) = -1.9\times 10^{3}~\textrm{kgm/s} \)

Reflection
The impulse is negative showing that the interaction causing the change in motion is opposite the initial direction of travel (the positive direction).

Problem D3.2

Problem D3.2

Problem

A 5.0E-3 kg particle is traveling in 1D with a speed of 335 m/s in the -z direction. After some elapsed time, the particle has a velocity of -875 m/s.

  1. What is the initial velocity of the particle?

  2. What is the initial and final momentums of the particle?

  3. Before calculating the impulse, make a prediction about the direction of impulse. Explain your reasoning.

  4. Calculate the momentum transfer for the particle. (Remember that impulse is momentum transfer.)

Methodology
With the 1D coordinate system given, we can assign directions through the +/- signs. Plug and chuck approach will allow us to compute the momentums and the impulse.

Solution
Part 1:

Since the speed is 335 m/s and it is traveling in the negative z-direction, the velocity must be

\(v_z = -335~\textrm{m/s}\)

Part 2:

The initial momentum is

\( p_{zi} = (5.0\times 10^{-3})(-335) = -1.7~\textrm{kgm/s} \)

The final momentum is

\( p_{zf} = (5.0\times 10^{-3})(-875) = -4.4~\textrm{kgm/s} \)

Part 3:

Since we are increasing the magnitude of momemtum in the negative direction, we must have an interaction directed in the negative direction. Answer: the momentum transfer (or impulse) is in the negative z-direction.

Part 4:

The impulse is the change in momentum:

\(\Delta p_z = p_{zf} - p_{zi} = (-4.4) - (-1.7) = -2.7~\textrm{kgm/s} \)

Reflection
The impulse is negative in agreement with our prediction. It is increasing the momentum by an amount of -2.7 kgm/s.

Problem D3.3

Problem D3.3

Problem
You observe a 2.0 kg point particle-like object initially moving at -45 m/s. The object is only subjected to a frictional force and comes to a stop after 8.0 s. What is the frictional force.

Methodology
Since the particle is only subject to the frictional force (or interaction), the frictional force must be the net force acting on the subject. We are also given indirectly that the final velocity of the particle is zero. With that information, we can employ to the impulse-momentum theorem and definition of momentum to find our answer:

\( \Delta p_x = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

combined with

\(\Delta p_x = mv_{fx} - mv_{ix} \)

Solution
Combining the two equations:

\(mv_{fx} - mv_{ix} = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

and solve for the net force:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{mv_{fx} - mv_{ix}}{\Delta t} \)

Now we can plug in our numbers to obtain the answer:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(2.0)(0) - (2.0)(-45)}{(8.0)} = +11~\textrm{N}\)

Reflection
The frictional force is positive. This makes sense: the particle is initially moving in the negative direction and to make it come to a stop, an interaction must act in the opposite direction of motion.

The problem could also have been solved using \( f_{net,x} = ma_x \) and using the velocities and elapsed time to find the acceleration.

Problem D3.4

Problem D3.4

Problem

Consider a 2.0 kg particle traveling at 25 m/s towards the fixed wall. The particle is subject to two different collisions with the same impact time of 0.020 s:

  • Head-on collision where the particle stick to the collision surface.

  • Head-on collision where the particle bounces of the collision surface with the same speed.

  1. Make a prediction to which of those two collisions the particle will experience the larger impact force.

  2. Quantify your prediction by calculating the impact force in both cases. Assume the impact force is much greater than any other forces involved in the collision.

Methodology

In the first case, we indirectly know that the final velocity is zero. We can combine the defition of momentum

\( p_x = mv_x \)

and the impulse-momentum theorem

\( \Delta p_x = f_{net,x}\Delta t \)

to obtain the impact force. Combining the two equations, we get for the net force (the same as the impact force, since any other forces are negligible):

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{mv_{fx} - mv_{ix}}{\Delta t} \)

Solution
In the following, we will make the initial direction of travel positive.
Part 1:

The impulse of the particle will be greater for the bouncing collision and therefore it will experience the greater impact force (more momentum transfer is needed).

Part 2:

Pluggin in values for the sticking collision:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(2.0)(0) - (2.0)(+25)}{2.0\times 10^{-2}} = -2.5\times 10^3~\textrm{N}\)

Pluggin in values for the bouncing collision:

\( f_{net,x} = \frac{(2.0)(-25) - (2.0)(+25)}{2.0\times 10^{-2}} = -5.0\times 10^3~\textrm{N}\)

Reflection
Our prediction was correct, the bouncing collision requires a greater momentum transfer and thus have a greater impact force (since both collisions takes place over the same time interval).

D5.2 Linear Work: Point Particle#

D5.2.1 Comparative Concept#

We previously explored the concept of linear impulse (\(j_x\)) as a change in linear momentum. This is also referred to as momentum transfer. This transfer of momentum required a net force (\(f_{{net}_x}\)) acting on the particle through some time and was quantified through

\[ j_x = f_{{net}_x}\Delta t \]

The key here is: the interaction is recorded over some time interval and associated with impulse (or change in momentum or momentum transfer).

D5.2.2 Linear Work: Point Particle#

Sometimes we may not have knowledge of the interaction (force) recorded over some time interval, but instead over some distance. For example, we may know that we are pushing a box with a force of 50 N a total distance of 15 m across a floor but do not know the time it took to do so.

We define the linear work done on the point particle as

Linear Work on Point Particle
\[ w = f_{{net}_x}\Delta x \tag{1}\]

where \(f_{{net}_x}\) is the net applied force on the particle and \(\Delta x\) is the directed distance the force is acting over. We notice that both the force and the distance are associated with directions through the \(x\)-subscript, and that will be clarified in more details in the next phase of the course. For now, we will apply the following rule:

  • If the applied force and direction of the motion are in same directions, then work is positive.
  • If the applied force and direction of motion are in opposite directions then work is negative.

    • We will see later that work is related to energy: work is transfer of energy. This transfer can take place within a system or between systems.

  • If work is positive: energy is transferred to the system by the net external force.
  • if work is negative: energy is removed from the system by the net external force.

    • Unit#

    The unit of work is Nm (Newton-meter) since we are multiplying force by distance. However, work is also energy transfer, and must therefore have units of J (Joule). We conclude the Nm = J.

    Example 1

    Problem
    A box is being pushed across the floor by a pushing force of 25 N. There is a frictional force of 5.0 N. After the box has been pushed a distance of 12 m, how much work was done on the box by the net force?

    Methodology
    We are given two forces acting on the system. The net force is the directed combination of those two forces, hence we can find the net force acting on the object. Knowing the net force and the directed distance, we can use Equation 1 to find the work done.

    Solution
    If we let the pushing force act in the \(+x\)-direction, then the net force is \(f_{{net}_x} = (+25) + (-5.0) = +20\) N and the work is

    \[ w = f_{{net}_x}\Delta x = (+20)(12) = +240~\textrm{Nm}\]

    Answer
    Since the forces and distance are given to two significant figures, we should communicate that properly in our answer:

    \[w = 2.4~\textrm{E}2~\textrm{Nm}\]

    Evaluation

    The since the net force and direction of motion are the same, the work is positive.
